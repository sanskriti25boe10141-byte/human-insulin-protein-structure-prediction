# Human Insulin Protein Structure Prediction using AlphaFold

## Project Overview

This project focuses on the structural analysis of Human Insulin using AlphaFold, an artificial intelligence-based protein structure prediction platform developed by DeepMind. The objective of this study is to visualize the three-dimensional structure of insulin and understand how protein structure contributes to its biological function.

Protein structure prediction is an important area of bioinformatics because the structure of a protein is closely related to its function. Understanding protein structures helps researchers in drug discovery, disease research, and molecular biology.

---

## Objectives

- Retrieve Human Insulin protein information from UniProt.
- Visualize the predicted 3D structure using AlphaFold.
- Analyze the structural characteristics of insulin.
- Understand the biological significance of protein structure.
- Document and interpret structural observations.

---

## Methodology

### Step 1: Protein Selection
Human Insulin (UniProt ID: P01308) was selected for structural analysis.

### Step 2: Structure Retrieval
The protein entry was searched in the AlphaFold Protein Structure Database.

### Step 3: Structure Visualization
The predicted three-dimensional structure was visualized using the integrated Mol* viewer available on the AlphaFold platform.

### Step 4: Structural Analysis
Different views of the protein structure were examined to understand its overall organization and structural features.

### Step 5: Documentation
Screenshots and observations were collected and organized for interpretation and reporting.

---

## Results and Observations

- The AlphaFold database successfully provided a predicted 3D structure of Human Insulin.
- The protein displayed a compact and organized structural arrangement.
- The predicted model provides valuable insight into the molecular architecture of insulin.
- Structural visualization helps in understanding how insulin performs its role in regulating blood glucose levels.
